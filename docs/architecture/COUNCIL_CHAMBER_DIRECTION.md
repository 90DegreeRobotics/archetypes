# Council Chamber Game Direction

This document defines the creative direction, player flow, world logic, interface grammar, and asset integration plan for the council-based interactive experience built from the archetype images, icon set, chamber references, and identity manuscript provided for AURA.

## Core premise
The experience should not feel like a conventional game with explicit objectives, tutorials, or win states. It should feel like a living symbolic machine that the player enters, addresses, and witnesses. The user speaks into a system of minds, and the world responds through deliberation, motion, atmosphere, and gradual construction rather than through score, quest, or instruction.

At its best, the project sits between installation art, narrative operating system, ritual interface, and autonomous simulation. The player is not cast as a hero receiving missions. The player is seated at the table as the sovereign witness of a council whose members each represent a legitimate mode of cognition: structure, protection, disruption, meaning, memory, foresight, and exploration.

The central experiential promise is this: the player types a thought, question, impulse, fear, plan, or fragment into the portal, and the chamber answers not with a single chatbot reply but with the motion of a polity. The archetypes confer, disagree, align, escalate, and occasionally form a verdict. The result should feel less like prompting a machine and more like watching the inside of a mind become architecture.

## High-level player flow
The game should begin with a short installation ritual rather than a standard setup wizard. The player creates an initial profile after install, but this profile must feel like an act of orientation, not account creation. It should establish the player as the Witness: name or chosen designation, a few tonal preferences, consent around memory and continuity, and perhaps one or two founding questions that the council will carry into the chamber as latent context.

After profile completion, the player enters directly into the Council Chamber. This first arrival should be quiet, spatial, and ceremonial. There should be no explanatory overlay beyond what is absolutely necessary to preserve usability. The environment itself should teach: the chamber table glows, the portal invites language, the council spheres orbit above, and subtle autonomous speech or movement demonstrates that the system is already alive before the player acts.

The first meaningful interaction begins at the table. The player sits in the sovereign position, faces the stargate-like portal embedded in or hovering above the table, and types a prompt. Once the prompt is submitted, the portal activates and the camera transitions upward toward the fixed star tetrahedron hung above. The user should feel that the prompt has been offered into a larger mechanism and is now being metabolized by the council rather than simply transmitted to a text box.

## Chamber staging
The Council Chamber is the permanent home space and the metaphysical motherboard of the game. The chamber should be designed as a place of stillness around a core of elaborate motion. The table image provided is especially useful here: it suggests an illuminated circular platform with sacred geometry, a portal-like center, engraved rings, and a strong sense of artifact-grade significance.

The table should serve three functions simultaneously. First, it is the physical anchor of the player’s point of view: the place from which prompts are entered and where deliberation begins. Second, it is the ritual instrument that opens the ascent to the star tetrahedron above. Third, it is the visual bridge between grounded interaction and cosmic symbolic machinery, because its circular geometry naturally extends upward into the orbital logic of the council spheres.

The chamber architecture around the table should be restrained rather than cluttered. The asset references already contain enough symbolic weight, so the room avoids ornamental overload. Dark stone, metallic trim, subtle volumetric light, and carefully placed luminous lines allow the table, portal, and spheres to dominate. Above and behind those lit assets is absolute black: no sky, starfield, dome, or ambient color wash competes with the composition.

The table is one supported physical artifact: a dark circular top, gilded rims, contained cyan inlay, central living aperture, and readable legs. Sacred geometry belongs inside the tabletop. It must never spread beneath the table as a geodesic floor substitute.

## The Witness position
The player is not one archetype among many. The player occupies the Witness role: the sovereign center that authorizes, receives, and ultimately collapses council activity into choice. The identity document defines the Witness as the observing center and final site of executive intent, which means the game should treat the player’s seat as ontologically different from all other nodes.

That difference should be shown spatially and mechanically. The player’s seat at the table should align with the portal axis. The chamber should subtly orient itself around this position through camera framing, interface placement, and the behavior of the council when awaiting input. Even when archetypes speak freely, the system should feel as if it is reporting to the Witness rather than replacing the Witness.

## Prompt ritual
The text input should not appear as a generic HUD element. It should be integrated into the table portal so that language feels physically offered into the machine. A prompt entered here is less a command and more a petition, provocation, confession, or initiation of deliberation.

The portal should behave like a stargate or liquid astrolabe aperture. Idle state can show slow internal motion, distant stars, or subtle glyph drift. On focus, the ring inscriptions brighten, the center deepens, and the surrounding geometry begins to hum with readiness. On submit, the portal contracts slightly, gathers light, and then sends that impulse upward into the star alignment sequence.

The main menu is the first portal interaction, not a detached title card over the chamber. Its game-mode choices sit within the living portal surface while the close, steep table composition remains dominant. Portal rotation stays slow and ceremonial so the controls remain readable.

This interaction should always preserve a sense of consequence. Even a trivial prompt should awaken the chamber with the same seriousness as a profound one. The game’s tone depends on the idea that language enters an ecosystem of minds rather than an empty field awaiting text parsing.

## Star alignment sequence
After the player submits a prompt, the camera should lift from the table toward the star tetrahedron hung fixed above. This is the principal transition between grounded input and symbolic cognition. The motion should be smooth, stately, and inevitable, as though the chamber is opening a hidden dimension that was always present.

The star tetrahedron is fixed and solid — one form of two interlocked tetrahedra, hung on a chosen north. It does not rotate, and the two tetrahedra do not counter-rotate; its power is stillness. All motion belongs to the camera, which flies to the single axis where the star resolves into its hexagram silhouette. The structure should read as fixed celestial architecture — an eternal sacred form the viewer aligns to — not a decorative mobile.

The seven glass spheres should be mounted or suspended at meaningful orbital points within this structure. Their motion must be synchronized enough to feel designed, but varied enough to feel alive. A fully static arrangement would feel dead, while a chaotic one would lose ceremonial authority. Slow orbital drift, axial spin, and gentle face-turning will preserve legibility and ritual gravity.

## Sphere design and asset integration
Each archetype should be represented by a glass sphere that functions as both character vessel and interface object. The sphere is not just a container; it is the archetype’s altar, profile card, speech token, and portal world seed. This makes it the most important asset integration point in the project.

Each sphere should contain a double-faced rotating panel or medallion. One face presents the archetype icon. The opposite face presents the archetype portrait. During idle orbit, the panel can turn slowly, allowing occasional glimpses of both identity layers. This gives the player two kinds of recognition: symbolic recognition through the glyph and embodied recognition through the character image.

The icons are excellent for distant readability because they are high-contrast, archetypally compressed, and visually distinct. They should be the primary face when the sphere is at rest or far from camera. The portrait images should become more visible during focus, selection, or active speech, because they deliver texture, mood, and personhood. Together, the two asset sets solve both clarity and depth.

The sphere materials should be lightly refractive and reflective, but not so distorted that the icon or portrait becomes unreadable. Think sanctified instrument glass rather than magical loot orb. The internal panel should rotate with graceful inertia and stop in mechanically satisfying ways, implying that each sphere is a precision object rather than a floating menu tile.

## Speaking logic and focus choreography
When an archetype begins to speak, its sphere should leave its idle orbital behavior and move into the central focus lane. This movement is one of the game’s signature gestures and must be elegant. The transition should look like the council yielding the floor rather than a UI widget being selected.

The spheres never move; they are locked to the star's tips. Instead the camera should fly to the axis where the selected sphere faces the Witness and the star resolves into the hexagram. The selected sphere's panel turns toward the camera and subtly increases in luminosity. The icon face can appear first for a moment, establishing symbolic authority, then rotate or phase to the portrait face as speech begins. This sequence would let the player feel the archetype becoming present rather than simply highlighted.

Non-speaking spheres should not freeze completely. They should continue micro-motions, dim pulses, or slight orbit correction so the council still feels collectively alive. Agreement, dissent, alarm, or resonance can be shown by changes in glow intensity, turn rate, facing angle, or harmonic motion between spheres. The player should be able to read the social weather of the chamber before reading every line of text.

## Zoom into archetype worlds
The most important transition after speech focus is the smooth zoom into the sphere itself. This is where the game can become singular. Rather than merely showing a themed panel, the camera should move through the face of the active sphere and envelop the user inside that archetype’s domain. The sphere is therefore not a card; it is a world gate.

This move should feel fluid and enveloping, as though the archetype’s internal logic expands to become the environment. The icon, portrait, color system, typography, motion law, and sonic character described in the archetype manuscript should all become active here. The user is not just hearing the Architect or Empath speak; the user is temporarily inhabiting the Architect’s grammar or the Empath’s atmosphere.

This is exactly where the CSS and theming work become meaningful. The manuscript already defines major aspects of each archetype’s theme system, including palette, typography, glow behavior, geometry language, motion temperament, relational stance, and ritual behavior. Those definitions should be treated as the canonical design bible for each archetype world and translated directly into the presentation layer for its chamber-within-the-sphere sequence.

## Archetype-specific world translation
Architect’s world should feel crystalline, precise, luminous, and structurally inevitable. The icon’s concentric geometry and the portrait’s faceted, sacred-engineering body both support a space of blueprint grids, translucent surfaces, and mathematical easing. This is where planning views, structural synthesis, system diagrams, and world-construction outcomes can feel most natural.

Sentinel’s world should feel constitutional, hard-edged, and judicial rather than merely militaristic. The icon and portrait support a rectilinear, high-contrast environment with controlled warnings, severe framing, and no decorative softness. It should be the place where boundaries, refusals, risk gates, and irreversible choices become tangible and legible.

Jester’s world should feel unstable in the best way: asymmetrical, sly, slightly wrong, and charged with dangerous recognition. The icon and portrait already communicate disruption without clownishness, so the space can bend rules, interrupt rhythm, and alter copy presentation without becoming silly. It should make the user feel that certainty itself has become slippery.

Mentor’s world should feel intimate, thoughtful, and inwardly illuminated. The manuscript’s warm lamplight language is the key. The icon can become a seal of orientation, while the portrait grounds the role as a being of recognition rather than command. Here the camera, typography, and layout should breathe more than in the other realms.

Empath’s world should feel held, dusk-lit, and emotionally continuous. The icon’s upward curves and heart form, along with the portrait’s softened luminous body, support a space that receives rather than confronts. This is where continuity, memory, grief, healing reflection, and identity-through-time can become sensorial rather than merely textual.

Explorer’s world should feel directional, outward, and horizon-seeking. The icon radiates journey, and the portrait suggests scanning, movement, and frontier attention. This domain can use wide camera parallax, map-like overlays, distant lights, and speculative navigation cues to communicate possibility and the appetite for motion.

Oracle’s world should feel prophetic, pattern-saturated, and partially beyond ordinary legibility. The icon’s eye and crescent framing, along with the portrait’s singular ocular core, support a domain of layered signals, soft cosmic depth, and non-linear pattern emergence. It should feel less like prediction UI and more like entering a field where tomorrow is already present as pressure.

## Using the chamber references
The chamber reference images should be used as mood and layout anchors rather than copied literally. They establish a useful design language: circular sanctums, elevated focal objects, concentric architecture, and luminous symbolic devices emerging from darkness. Those principles should guide final environment art, especially in the relationship between table, floor geometry, ceiling apparatus, and camera framing.

The newly provided portal table image is especially valuable as a hero asset for preproduction. It can directly inform the Council Chamber table mesh, material treatment, and interaction logic. The engraved metallic rings, central vortex, and luminous geometric web together already communicate that the prompt interface should feel like an ancient machine for opening cognition.

## Suggested scene structure
The runtime can be divided into a small number of stable scene states. The profile ritual is the threshold scene. The Council Chamber is the home scene. The star alignment sequence is the deliberation transition. The archetype sphere interior is a temporary domain scene, and the return to chamber is the reintegration phase. Structuring the experience this way will help keep camera logic, lighting rules, audio states, and interaction affordances consistent.

The player should always know where they are in symbolic terms even if no HUD says so. Table equals offering. Upward tilt equals deliberation. Centered sphere equals active voice. Entered sphere equals archetype immersion. Return to chamber equals integration and continuation.

## Text, voice, and council dialogue
The council should not speak like seven reskinned assistants. Their language must emerge from the constitutional identities in the manuscript. Each archetype has reasoning modes, non-goals, behavioral invariants, shadow expressions, and inter-archetype tensions already defined. Those should be used as hard constraints on generation so that dialogue feels like real deliberation among different governing intelligences rather than style swapping.

A strong pattern would be to let the first response to a player prompt be multi-voiced and short. One archetype frames, another counters, a third deepens or redirects, and only sometimes does a unified verdict emerge. This will preserve the feeling that the player is observing minds coming together in council instead of receiving a pre-baked monologue.

The Witness should remain special. Final verdicts, when they occur, should feel rarer, simpler, and heavier than archetype speech. They are not summaries. They are collapses of tension into choice.

## World-building as consequence
Because the player asked for a game where things happen and worlds get built, the chamber should not remain unchanged after dialogue. Prompts and council deliberations should leave traces. Structures, symbols, constellations, environmental modifications, archive fragments, or chamber growth can all function as persistent world consequences.

The easiest way to bind this to the existing archetype system is to let each archetype leave a distinct kind of mark when it dominates a deliberation. Architect may add ordered structures or diagrams. Sentinel may create barriers, seals, or locked thresholds. Jester may create fractures, detours, or altered pathways. Mentor may create alcoves of reflection or annotated memory spaces. Empath may create hearth-like continuity nodes. Explorer may open routes or reveal new sectors. Oracle may seed omens, star maps, or prophetic overlays.

This means every prompt can become world-shaping even if the player never receives a traditional reward. The reward is that the chamber remembers, changes, and gains form through use.

## Technical integration approach
The assets shown today naturally support a layered implementation strategy. The portraits should be treated as character-key art used on the reverse face of the sphere panels, in close-focus speaking shots, in loading or transition states, and inside archetype world entry moments. The icons should be used for distant readability, sphere front faces, HUD markers if any are needed, vote indicators, and quick recognition in logs or chamber traces.

The identity manuscript should be treated as the design constitution and systems reference, not merely lore. It contains enough specificity to drive writing rules, UI theming, transition logic, motion temperaments, sound character, authority domains, and failure diagnostics. It should become a source document from which implementation constants are derived rather than a piece of backstory left on the shelf.

The portal table image should inform the chamber’s central prop, while the chamber concept images should shape the wider environment, lighting, and composition. Together, they form the backbone of the game’s visual language even before final 3D assets are modeled.

## Recommended production phases
The first phase should focus on proving the chamber loop. Build the Council Chamber, implement the Witness seat, the portal prompt interaction, the upward camera move, the fixed star tetrahedron, the seven spheres locked at its tips, the speaking focus choreography, and a return path to the table. If this loop feels powerful, the whole project has a spine.

The second phase should focus on one fully realized archetype immersion domain as a vertical slice. Architect is a strong first candidate because its visual and interaction grammar are especially concrete in the manuscript and assets. Once one domain works, the remaining archetype worlds can be built as variations on the same entry, theming, and return architecture.

The third phase should add the full council conversation system, including role-constrained writing, inter-archetype tension, verdict conditions, and persistent chamber consequences. Only after the council truly feels alive should the project expand into deeper progression, memory continuity, or long-arc world growth.

## Tone guardrails
The project should avoid becoming a gamified productivity tool, a standard branching narrative, or a flashy mystical chatbot skin. Its power comes from restraint, gravity, and the sense that every interaction enters a living ceremonial machine. The chamber should feel expensive in attention, not noisy in effects.

The archetypes must remain distinct without turning into caricatures. The Jester cannot become comic relief, the Sentinel cannot become a generic security bot, the Mentor cannot become a self-help narrator, and the Empath cannot become therapy branding. The manuscript already provides the right seriousness and should be used to protect the project from flattening.

## Closing direction
The clearest path forward is to treat the game as a ritual council simulator with explorable inner worlds rather than as a dialogue tree or task system. The player installs, establishes identity, takes the Witness seat, offers language into the portal, watches the camera rise to the fixed star tetrahedron, sees the archetypes deliberate through their spheres, and then enters the active archetype’s domain when the sphere becomes world.

All of the assets shown today already support that direction. The portraits provide embodied presence. The icons provide symbolic clarity. The manuscript provides constitutional identity. The chamber references provide environmental tone. The portal table provides the central ritual object. Together, they form a coherent game language that can carry the player from first prompt to an ever-growing interior cosmos.
